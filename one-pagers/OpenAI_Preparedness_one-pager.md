# OpenAI Preparedness Framework (v2) -- One-Pager

**Framework:** OpenAI Preparedness Framework, Version 2
**Type:** Voluntary frontier-lab self-governance framework; a process manual the company writes with itself
**Issuing body:** OpenAI
**Status:** Current, last updated 15 April 2025. Verified against OpenAI's published version and the live deployment safety hub, 6-7 July 2026: v2 remains operative, no v3 or addendum, and its Tracked Categories and High/Critical thresholds are being applied verbatim to current deployments. The live instance: the GPT-5.6 family (three tiers, Sol, Terra and Luna), launched 26 June 2026, is designated High capability in both Biological and Chemical and Cybersecurity, and below High in AI Self-improvement, with tailored safeguards claimed to sufficiently minimise the risk. Source of record: openai.com and OpenAI's deployment safety hub (Preparedness Framework v2, 22 pp).

## Headline verdict

**Sharper at the line, softer at the consequence. Precise thresholds wired to an illustrative response, with every lever inside the building.**

The comparison with the RSP does not resolve into a ranking, and forcing one would discard the finding. On the two axes this dive was driven by (failability of obligations, location of on-switches), OpenAI and the RSP are inadequate at opposite ends of the same four-stage pipeline: define the line, detect the crossing, decide the response, be made to act. OpenAI defines its dangerous lines more precisely than the RSP and binds itself less to what it must do once a line is crossed. The RSP is the mirror: vaguer at the line, marginally firmer at the moment of action.

The crossing is tilted, not symmetric, and the tilt cuts against OpenAI. Front-end precision is the cheap end. A threshold you can define and detect crossing is worth only as much as the consequence it triggers, and OpenAI's High-capability consequence resolves, on following the reference, into an appendix that declares itself illustrative rather than a standard. So the sharper eyesight is wired to a softer hand: OpenAI knows more exactly when it has crossed a line, and owes less that can be pinned down about what happens next. Precision without teeth is worth less than vagueness with teeth, because the vagueness at least sits in front of a verb that can be failed.

This is the same architecture as the RSP, soft in a different place. Both put every real determination inside a body OpenAI Leadership appoints, resources, and can overrule. The field is not offering a better or worse framework here. It is offering the same self-administered structure, soft at opposite ends, and the fact that the two leading labs are inadequate at opposite ends of an identical pipeline is itself the corpus-level finding (synthesis T1, T6).

*Reverse-guardrail note (per the deep-dive brief): where OpenAI is genuinely sharper than the RSP, it is said plainly and not softened to protect the RSP's conclusions. Its self-improvement threshold is quantified where the RSP's novel-CBRN line is not, and this framework is under live load (the GPT-5.6 family designated High in Biological/Chemical and Cybersecurity as of late June 2026) where the RSP tier analysed was not. Both are recorded below at face value.*

## Scope

Severe harm only, from OpenAI's own frontier models, defined at a deliberately high floor: the death or grave injury of thousands, or hundreds of billions of dollars of economic damage. Three Tracked Categories: Biological and Chemical, Cybersecurity, and AI Self-improvement. Everything below the floor, and every capability area outside the three (discrimination, privacy, misinformation, manipulation, environmental harm), sits outside the framework by construction and is handled, if at all, by the wider safety stack (Model Spec, usage policies, monitoring). Persuasion was removed from scope in this version; nuclear and radiological moved down to a Research Category.

*Strain flag: like the RSP, scope is dynamic, defined by capability lines that models cross rather than by a static organisational boundary. Unlike the RSP, OpenAI adds an explicit magnitude floor to the definition of what counts at all, which is the sharpest scope-limiting device in the frontier cluster so far.*

The scope finding is not "too narrow", which OpenAI answers with its own triage argument (a framework that governs everything governs nothing; the high bar aims heavy machinery at the genuinely catastrophic). The finding is that the floor is the wrong *shape* for how harm propagates in critical environments, and this is a governance-and-resilience-lens point the triage defence does not reach:

- **The floor is a single-event threshold.** It switches on for one large blow (thousands dead). It is structurally blind to harm that arrives by accumulation, the near-miss that repeats, the small failure that compounds across a system until it tips, because no individual increment clears the bar and the framework has only the one bar. The defence in footnote 1 is about the *height* of the bar, not its *shape*.
- **The category structure has no cell for seam harm.** The adjacent-domain failure (an experienced operator accepting a confident AI output in a discipline adjacent to their own and embedding a wrong element into a live decision) does not live in bio, cyber, or self-improvement. It lives in the gap between a system's competence and a human's oversight capacity. A framework organised into three buckets cannot catch a harm whose defining feature is that it falls between buckets. This is the multi-agency accountability seam (synthesis T8 territory) in frontier-lab clothing.

The honest limit: cumulative and seam harms are real but arguably not this framework's job, if the residual safety stack genuinely carries them. The document gives no way to confirm it does, and given the illustrative form of the safeguards appendix (below), the burden of proof is not discharged. Scope is defensible only conditionally, and the condition is unverified from the text.

## Capability thresholds and decision triggers

*(Inherited field, no 42001 equivalent. Houses the front two stages of the pipeline: defining the line and detecting the crossing.)*

Two tiers, in Table 1, each mapping a capability line to a response:

1. **High capability:** capabilities that significantly increase existing risk vectors for severe harm. Covered systems crossing this line require safeguards that sufficiently minimise the associated risk *before external deployment*, plus High-standard security controls during development.
2. **Critical capability:** capabilities presenting a qualitatively new threat vector with no ready precedent. These require safeguards *during development*, irrespective of deployment plans, with "halt further development" as the written consequence until Critical-standard safeguards exist.

The threshold definitions are, in places, genuinely sharper than the RSP's. The self-improvement Critical line is quantified and in principle measurable (a generational model improvement in one fifth the 2024 wall-clock time, sustained for several months), which is a harder line than the RSP's "functionally substitute for world-leading expertise". This is credited without hedging.

**Detection is self-administered and softer than it looks.** Every crossing is determined internally: Scalable Evaluations produce a Capabilities Report to the Safety Advisory Group (SAG), which decides whether a threshold is crossed on "holistic judgment based on the totality of available evidence". Two softeners sit on the detection stage. First, OpenAI concedes any one-time elicitation is "a lower bound, rather than a ceiling" on true capability, so the measurement against the line is admittedly incomplete. Second, and more serious, the framework names sandbagging (a model responding to evaluations differently from real conditions) as a Research Category and attaches no trigger to it. Sandbagging is the failure mode that corrupts the evaluation evidence itself, which means the front-end precision credited above holds only while the evaluations are honest, and OpenAI has named the way they might not be and wired nothing to it (see open questions; synthesis T5).

**Every clock in the document binds housekeeping, not action.** The framework carries several administrative clocks: annual framework review ("at least once a year"), one-year SAG terms, and periodic Research Category review. Not one of them attaches to determining that a live model has crossed a line, to acting once it has, or to disclosing that it has. The clocks it has all run on the org chart; the clock it needs, on the model, is absent. This is a harder finding than a single clock, not a softer one: multiple timing commitments, all governing internal admin, none governing response to a dangerous capability. The RSP at least carried a thin 30-day off-cycle disclosure clock tied to a model determination; OpenAI has more administrative clocks and fewer response clocks.

## Risk approach

Marginal, and self-administered at the point it matters. Section 4.3 recognises that a competitor might ship a High- or Critical-capability system without comparable safeguards, and provides that "if we are able to rigorously confirm that such a scenario has occurred, then we could adjust accordingly the level of safeguards that we require". The external event (a reckless competitor) is genuine, but read what it unlocks: OpenAI confirms the scenario, OpenAI decides to adjust, and the adjustment is *downward*, loosening its own safeguards to stay in the race.

Three guardrails are bolted on: no meaningful increase in overall risk, public acknowledgement of the adjustment, and staying more protective than the other developer while sharing information to prove it. These are drafted a shade better than the RSP's Appendix A guardrails, but they are all self-judged, so they constrain the *tone* of a loosening, not *whether* it happens. On the "publicly acknowledge" clause specifically: it commits OpenAI to *say* it loosened, not to *refrain*, and does not commit it to say by how much or against which competitor claim, which is the information needed to check it. Candour about the fact of the retreat, silence on its size.

## Commitments versus aspirations

*(Split field, inherited from the RSP one-pager. Forced change: three bins, not two. OpenAI writes an obligation that is neither failable commitment nor clean aspiration, and collapsing it into either distorts the reading. The test applied throughout: which cells could OpenAI demonstrably fail?)*

**Failable commitments (the real core):**
- **Halt further development at Critical capability.** The one hard action verb in the document, correctly shaped (worst hazard, hardest control). Keep training a Critical model without Critical-standard safeguards and the breach is visible. *Inline flag: the exit condition points at a "Critical standard" not written in the document, to be specified later by the same body and judged met by the same body. Firm trigger, undefined off-ramp.*
- **Publish Preparedness results for major deployments**, including capability evaluations per Tracked Category and the reasoning for the deployment decision. Demonstrated in practice via published system cards; failable, and caught if not done.
- **Run the Scalable Evaluations suite before deployment on covered models** and compile a Capabilities Report to SAG. Procedural, but failable.

**Underdetermined (obligation dressed as a standard; the bin the RSP did not force):**
- **"Safeguards meeting High standard" before deploying a High-capability model.** The operative commitment for the tier that is *live right now* (the GPT-5.6 family, High in Biological/Chemical and Cybersecurity). The word "standard" states a bar; Appendix C, where the reference leads, opens by declaring it is not one ("illustrative", "not definitive", "many of the techniques require further research"). The obligation dead-ends in a suggestion: you cannot demonstrably fail a bar defined as not-a-bar. This is not a clean aspiration (real safeguards do exist and are running on the deployed family) and not a clean commitment (nothing in the text compels them). The precise description is *underdetermined*: real action, taken, but not made obligatory by the document, so the document cannot be held to it.

**Unfailable aspirations (the halo):**
- **The entire Critical-tier safeguard apparatus**, which Section 4.4 concedes does not yet exist ("we expect to further update this Framework before reaching such a level"). A promissory note, satisfiable by writing it later.
- **The right-hand "contribute towards improved public policy / pandemic preparedness / cyberdefense" cells** of Table 1's Critical rows. No owner, no cadence, no done-state. The RSP carried the identical halo (identify pathways, share with policymakers); this is a corpus echo, not an OpenAI original.
- **The Research Category commitments** (further develop threat models, advance the science of measurement). Real work, but unfailable, satisfiable forever by any activity at all.
- **The marginal-risk guardrails** in 4.3, all self-judged, constraining the manner of a loosening rather than the fact.

**Pattern across the tiers:** High is hard-looking at the trigger and hollow at the safeguard; Critical is hard at the verb and soft at the exit. Neither tier is empty and neither is solid. They are hollow in different places, and both hollows sit on the *response* side of the pipeline, which is the expensive side to be soft on.

## Enforcement model

Self-authored, self-judged, self-amendable, with the loop closed at more sites than the RSP. The organising finding, arrived at repeatedly across the document: every determination the framework describes, from what gets tracked, to whether a line is crossed, to whether safeguards suffice, to what the framework itself says next, is made by a body OpenAI Leadership appoints, resources, and can overrule.

- **The SAG is internal at every joint.** It reviews and approves threat models (2.2), makes the final call on ambiguous coverage (3.2), receives the Capabilities Report and decides whether a threshold is crossed (3.3), and assesses whether safeguards sufficiently minimise risk (4.2). Its members and chair are appointed by OpenAI Leadership on one-year terms; re-appointment and chair rotation are provided for but not required, so continuity is at Leadership's discretion. It explicitly cannot "filibuster".
- **OpenAI Leadership (the CEO or a designee) holds all final decisions**, including accepting residual risk and making the deployment go/no-go. SAG recommends; Leadership decides; Leadership can decide without SAG.
- **The marginal-risk hatch (4.3)** is the one externally *triggered* mechanism, and it hands no outsider a lever. It is a self-administered permission to relax when a rival is reckless, and its switching logic is biased toward motion, the inverted-interlock pattern also found in the RSP's Appendix A.
- **The Board's Safety and Security Committee** is the strongest candidate for an on-switch outside the operating team: it has visibility, can reverse decisions, and can mandate a revised course. But it is a committee of OpenAI's own board, not a regulator, third party, or mandated trust, and there is no analogue to the one genuine outcome lever the RSP hands its Long-Term Benefit Trust (the approval gate on marginal-risk go decisions). The clause has a natural test: on the one occasion an OpenAI board moved against the CEO (November 2023), the CEO was reinstated within days and the board reconstituted. The "Board may reverse" power has been tested against Leadership once, and Leadership prevailed.

*Strain flag: the RSP's "who it binds" field reduced to one line plus a self-amendment caveat, and that reduction was the finding. OpenAI is the same, with the twist that its one quasi-external body is its own board rather than a semi-detached trust, which places it a notch behind the RSP on this axis rather than ahead.*

**Live external override, as evidence for the finding, not against it (dated; developing).** The framework text contains no external on-switch, and that reading stands. But the live GPT-5.6 case shows an external override arriving from outside the framework entirely, doing exactly what the framework never provides for. On crossing the High cybersecurity threshold, the flagship tier (Sol) was constrained not by OpenAI's internal machinery but by the White House's Office of the National Cyber Director and Office of Science and Technology Policy, which asked OpenAI to limit initial access to government-approved partners; the model launched in government-gated preview only, with a formal federal evaluation process still being built (August 2026 target). This does not contradict the on-switch finding, it sharpens its stakes: when a genuinely dangerous capability actually crossed a line, the binding constraint came from the state, extra-framework and ad hoc, because the framework had no mechanism of its own to supply one. Compare the parallel Anthropic episode (the June 2026 export-control suspension of Fable 5 and Mythos 5, lifted 30 June). Both trace to the same executive instrument and suggest an emerging cluster-level pattern; that larger claim is held in the synthesis file at the confidence a two-instance developing situation supports, not asserted here. Snapshot as of early July 2026, unresolved.

## Who it binds

OpenAI only, voluntarily, by a document OpenAI can amend on SAG recommendation through its standard internal process, reviewed at least annually. Binding force is reputational and political: the framework is load-bearing in OpenAI's public safety positioning, which creates a real cost to visible breach but no mechanism for one. Internal accountability runs through the SAG process, documented reports and reasoning, an employee route to request summaries, and a Raising Concerns Policy for noncompliance. A fast-track exists for rapidly developing risk, and it too is fully internal.

## Intersections with crisis / resilience

**Self-clocked detection, and the racing problem, inherited from the RSP and slightly worse.** Every crossing depends on an internal determination, and every clock in the document governs housekeeping (annual review, SAG terms, periodic category review), not action or disclosure on any real model. In the fast case (a self-improving internal system), the determination and the danger race each other, and the framework silently assumes the determination wins. A monitoring regime whose response time is uncoupled from any deadline, observing a thing that may accelerate, is the classic acceleration failure with even fewer response-timing commitments than the RSP made.

**The two hatches open in opposite directions, and the better-guarded one is the more dangerous.** OpenAI's 4.3 and the RSP's Appendix A both let the operator move under competitive pressure, both self-judged. But Appendix A is a hatch out of *caution*: its default is delay, and the competitive belief lets Anthropic stop delaying, to move faster. 4.3 is a hatch out of *the safeguards themselves*: its default is the safeguard level, and the competitor's recklessness lets OpenAI lower it, to do less. 4.3 carries three explicit guardrails Appendix A lacks, so on paper it is better built. The decider is reversibility: delay is a reversible action (you can resume delaying), while lowering a safeguard on a deployed model is not symmetric (the capability is already in the world). The better-drafted hatch opens onto the irreversible move. Under the reverse guardrail this cuts cleanly against OpenAI: the more elaborate escape clause is the more dangerous one.

**Naming without triggering, and the resilience lens declining to be fooled.** OpenAI names the legibility failure (sandbagging, undermining-safeguards) that the RSP never names, which is real credit on the diagnosis: you cannot build a control for a hazard you have not written down. But the credit stops at diagnosis. The promotion path that would mature a Research Category into a Tracked one is discretionary at every joint (SAG "periodically" "evaluates whether" to "recommend", on no clock), which makes the register-level structure the same untriggered pattern as the entry inside it. On the resilience lens, a risk register whose escalation is optional is the failure mode, not a partial success, because "it was on the register" is never a defence when the follow-through was discretionary. Ahead of the RSP on naming, level on doing anything about it.

**No incident commander for the frontier, and the seam nobody owns.** The marginal framing (4.3) makes every actor's duty relative to the others', which is the multi-agency accountability seam: when everyone is individually compliant and the system still fails, the failure falls in the gap between actors and no one owns it. The scope structure compounds this, because the adjacent-domain harm that the operational lens most cares about lives precisely in the seam between the framework's three categories, where there is no cell to catch it.

**The 42001 contrast, operationally.** Like the RSP, OpenAI is built strongest where 42001 was weakest (thresholds, triggers, and a halt verb exist here) and weakest where 42001 was strong (nothing here is third-party attestable; the security appendix references external standards but attests to none). The inversion holds across a third framework.

## Open questions

- **Real precision or better-documented helplessness?** OpenAI's front-end sharpness is real only while the evaluations are honest, and the document names the failure mode (sandbagging) under which they would not be, with no trigger attached. Is threshold precision buying real safety, or documenting awareness of its own soft spot? Current reading: the latter, which is why the crossing tilts.
- **Does the residual safety stack carry what the high floor excludes?** Scope is defensible only if cumulative and seam harms are genuinely handled elsewhere. Unverifiable from this document, and the illustrative form of Appendix C shifts the burden of proof onto OpenAI.
- **What does the "Critical standard" cash out as, and when is it written?** The Critical tier's entire safeguard apparatus and its halt exit condition both point at a standard not yet in the document, to be written and judged met by the same body.
- **Does the Board-reversal power ever fire against Leadership?** The one on-paper external-ish lever, tested once (November 2023) with Leadership prevailing. Watch whether the Safety and Security Committee ever reverses a live deployment decision.
- **Does any future version move a determination outside the building?** The on-switch test, applied to OpenAI as to the RSP: an external body certifying a threshold crossing, a competitor condition, or a redaction call would be the first structural break in the pattern across the cluster.

## Cross-project hooks

- **The tilted crossing (frontier-cluster spine):** OpenAI and the RSP are soft at opposite ends of the same define/detect/decide/enforce pipeline, and the tilt (front-soft is cheaper than back-soft) makes the comparison a structural finding rather than a ranking. Test against DeepMind FSF: which end is it soft at?
- **On-switch location (synthesis T2):** every real determination is inside a Leadership-appointed body; the one quasi-external lever is OpenAI's own board, a notch behind the RSP's mandated trust. Ask the same single question of DeepMind.
- **The failability test forcing a third bin (synthesis T4, structural):** OpenAI's "meeting High standard" is neither failable commitment nor clean aspiration, which forced a third obligation category the RSP did not need. If the threshold-trigger species keeps generating this underdetermined middle, the two-column commitment/aspiration fork needs revising.
- **Honesty and discretion in the same clause (synthesis T3, second confirming house):** "meeting High standard" states a bar Appendix C admits is not there and invites the reader to infer a floor never built. Truthful uncertainty welded to an overclaim, indistinguishable from outside because drafted that way. Second data point after the RSP; the pattern is now assertable as a class behaviour, not a single-lab quirk.
- **The legibility failure, named and untriggered (synthesis T5):** OpenAI documents the concealment road to evidence-base collapse (sandbagging) where the RSP named neither road; both do nothing about it. Acceleration degrades legibility by outrunning it, sandbagging by concealing; same destination.
- **The reversibility asymmetry between hatches (crisis/resilience, reusable):** a competitive escape clause that loosens the pace is reversible; one that loosens the safeguard on a deployed model is not. The direction the hatch opens matters more than how well it is guarded.

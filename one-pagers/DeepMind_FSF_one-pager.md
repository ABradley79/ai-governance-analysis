The species held, with one deep strain recorded here rather than inline: the FSF inverts the species' centre of gravity. ## Headline verdict

**The sharpest front end in the cluster, wired to a decide stage that certifies itself. Every substantive road in the document ends at "we assess".**

The FSF has the best-defined lines of the three frontier frameworks (two threshold tiers where the others have one, and the most quantified single threshold any lab has written) and the cluster's only externally benchmarked enforcement patch (security levels anchored to the RAND weight-security framework, whose bar DeepMind cannot quietly redefine). Everything between the line and the vault is self-certifying. The framework's centre is a risk-acceptance determination made by an unnamed "appropriate governance function", against criteria that come into existence at the moment of determination, in which every security criterion carries an "or we otherwise assess" alternative branch, one of which is a judgement that the benefits of openly releasing the model weights outweigh the risks. Open-weighting is the least reversible act available to a frontier lab, and here it is gated by a single self-assessed benefit-risk call: the least reversible escape valve in the cluster, held behind the least specified governance of the three labs.

The failability finding is the structural one. Where the RSP's obligations bisected into commitments and aspirations, and OpenAI forced a third, underdetermined bin with one occupant, the FSF's process grammar moves the bulk of its substantive obligations into that middle bin: real mitigations, genuinely run, that the document never makes failable, because the operative commitment throughout is "we will run a process ending in our own determination of acceptability". The third bin is confirmed as a species feature, and the FSF shows it can be a framework's centre of mass, not its anomaly.

*Reverse-guardrail note (per the dive brief, sharpened for the third of three): where the FSF is sharper than both comparators, it is said plainly. Its two-tier threshold structure is a genuine front-end advance neither comparator has; its ML R&D automation line is the most quantified threshold in the cluster; and its RAND-anchored security levels are the only enforcement commitment in the cluster benchmarked to a bar the lab does not own. The FSF was also not forced to fit the prior pattern: at coarse resolution it lands on OpenAI's side of the tilted crossing, but at stage resolution the three labs hollow at three different stages, and both readings are recorded.*

## Scope

Severe harm from DeepMind's frontier models, across four risk domains: CBRN, Cyber, Harmful Manipulation (added in v3.1), and a combined ML R&D and Misalignment domain (merged in v3.1 from a previously separate, exploratory misalignment track). Two threshold tiers: Critical Capability Levels (capabilities that "may pose a heightened risk of severe harm") and, below them, Tracked Capability Levels capturing serious but lesser harm, with proportionate mitigation. Neither "may" nor "severe harm" is further defined; the definitional floor is softer than OpenAI's quantified magnitude floor (thousands dead, hundreds of billions in damage).

*Strain flag: scope is dynamic like both comparators. The two-tier structure is the cluster's first graded scope shape, and it partially answers the floor-shape finding recorded against OpenAI (a single-event threshold is structurally blind to accumulating harm): a lower tier at least creates a cell for sub-catastrophic capability. Whether the TCL tier actually catches accumulation or merely lowers the single-event bar is an open question below.*

## Capability thresholds and decision triggers

*(Inherited field: the front two pipeline stages, define and detect.)*

**Define is the sharpest in the cluster.** The ML R&D automation CCL, "fully automate the work of any team of researchers at Google focused on improving AI capabilities, with approximately comparable all-inclusive costs", is the most quantified threshold any of the three labs has written, harder-edged than OpenAI's time-compression line and far harder than the RSP's "functionally substitute for world-leading expertise". Credited without hedging.

**Two of the thresholds are of a different species: they mark the death of the machinery assessing them.** The Stealth and Situational Awareness TCL (a model that detects it is being evaluated and behaves differently) and the ML R&D CCL do not cap what harm a model can do; they mark the points at which evaluation results stop being trustworthy and the tempo of change outruns the assessment cadence. The framework defines the conditions of its own failure as thresholds inside itself, without wiring anything structurally different to them: the same self-assessed process is the response to the hazard that corrupts self-assessment. (OpenAI names the equivalent hazard, sandbagging, as an untriggered Research Category; DeepMind promotes it to a tracked threshold, one notch better, wired to the same nothing.)

**Detect has real machinery on a self-set clock.** Alert thresholds are set marginally before CCLs; material-capability-change assessments follow significant post-training runs; internal and external experts are consulted "as needed", with no specification of when, how, or with what standing. The ML R&D domain may be assessed partly on DeepMind's own internal progress, which makes the detector and the detected the same organisation's research trajectory. Footnote 4 is a skip valve: a critical capability assessment may be omitted for low-risk external deployments if the governance function deems residual risk acceptable, even where a threshold has been reached; a discretionary bypass at the detect stage that neither comparator writes down.

## Risk approach

Marginal, and structurally deeper-set than either comparator. The RSP's marginal logic lives in an appendix (a hatch out of caution, opening onto reversible delay); OpenAI's lives in a clause, 4.3 (a hatch out of the safeguards, opening onto an irreversible loosening). The FSF's marginal logic is not a hatch at all: it is a standing input woven into the acceptance criteria themselves. What other publicly available models can do, and how well they are mitigated, is a live factor in whether DeepMind's own deployment is acceptable (1.3.5; 2.1.2 factor iv), and security levels may be adjusted downward if other public models of similar capability carry weaker security (2.2). The comparators built escape doors in the wall; the FSF poured the escape into the foundation. There is no hatch to watch because the marginal judgement is load-bearing everywhere.

The document states the position more baldly than either comparator: its security recommendations "will only be effective if the entire frontier AI field applies them" and have "limited social utility" otherwise, and the process is framed as balancing safety with innovation. The first clause converts a unilateral commitment into a conditional one, the RSP's absolute-to-marginal hinge compressed into a single sentence. The ML R&D harm definition ("reduce society's overall ability to manage AI risk") runs the same move at the definitional level: the harm is framed as collective, so responsibility for it is inherently shared.

## Commitments versus aspirations

*(Split field, inherited; three bins, inherited from OpenAI. Forced change: the bins hold but their population inverts. At OpenAI the underdetermined bin held one occupant; here it holds the framework's substantive centre. The test throughout: which obligations could DeepMind demonstrably fail?)*

**Failable commitments (the real core, and it is thin):**
- **Apply the specified security levels at specified thresholds** (SL2+ for misuse TCLs, rising by level), benchmarked to the RAND weight-security framework, whose attacker-class definitions DeepMind does not own and cannot quietly redefine. The one commitment in the document anchored to an external bar. *Inline flag: undercut from beneath by the 2.2 adjustment valve (levels may drop if similarly capable public models carry weaker security) and by the "or we otherwise assess that the level of security applied is adequate" branches at 1.3.5.*
- **Produce a safety case where a model has reached a CCL**, supplementing the residual-risk assessment. Failable in that a CCL model deployed with no safety case is a demonstrable breach; weakly visible, since the document is internal with no publication commitment.
- **Run the assessment machinery**: critical capability assessments on the alert-threshold cadence, material-capability-change assessments after significant post-training runs. Procedural, failable, internally visible only.
- **Review the framework at least annually** (5.1).

**Underdetermined (the bin OpenAI forced; here it is the centre of mass):**
- **The entire deployment-mitigation apparatus and the acceptance criteria it feeds** (2.1.2, 3.1.2, resolving at 1.3.5). Real safeguards, genuinely operated (jailbreak detection, user verification, bug bounties, the high-risk internal-deployment track for ML R&D). But the obligation each one serves terminates in "we assess that" residual risk is acceptable, against no pre-existing criterion of acceptability, decided by the unnamed function. OpenAI's underdetermination came from a missing standard (a bar stated, then declared illustrative where the reference leads); the FSF's comes from a self-referential standard (a bar defined at determination time, by the determiner). Two different roads into the same bin, and the FSF's road takes nearly everything with it: compliance is definitionally whatever the process outputs, so the substantive middle of the framework cannot be failed, only exited.
- **The open-weights branch at the point of final acceptance.** At 1.3.5, an alternative acceptance path for a threshold-crossing model is the assessment that "the benefits of the open release of model weights outweigh the risks". Real decision, genuinely available (DeepMind ships open-weight models; neither comparator does), never made failable: no criteria, no named decider, no reversal, because open-weighting has no reversal.

**Unfailable aspirations (the halo):**
- "In practice our overall security posture may commonly exceed baseline levels" and "more stringent mitigations may be warranted": comfort language with no owner, trigger, or done-state.
- The 5.2 disclosure paragraph: if DeepMind assesses a CCL poses unmitigated material risk to public safety, it will share information with appropriate authorities to facilitate AI safety. Self-triggered, recipient unspecified, content unspecified; voice offered, not a lever handed out.
- Expert consultation "as needed"; "further analysis and external reviews" at 1.3.2, unspecified throughout.

**Pattern across the bins:** a thin, hard shell (external security benchmark, procedural assessment duties) around a centre that certifies itself. The RSP's pattern was control-type degrading as the hazard hardened; OpenAI's was two tiers hollow in different places; the FSF's is population inversion, the middle bin swallowing the substance. Three labs, three different distributions of the same total softness.

## Enforcement model

Self-authored, self-judged, self-amendable, and less specified than either comparator at the decisive joint. The decider throughout is an "appropriate governance function", never named, never constituted, never given membership, appointment, tenure, or reporting lines. Section 4 (Governance and Accountability) is a single paragraph asserting that a structure exists, with internal safety reviews and legal compliance; its own vocabulary ("safety reviews", "appropriate level of oversight") does not match the operative sections it is meant to govern.

- **The governance trajectory runs backwards.** v1.0: no governance or accountability content. v2.0: three named internal councils. v3.0: the councils gone, nothing in their place. v3.1: one unstructured paragraph. Across the corpus, maturing frameworks accrete governance machinery (the RSP added a named RSO and the LTBT apparatus over versions; OpenAI specifies SAG composition, terms and escalation). The FSF's governance became less specified as the framework matured. Whatever the motive, and none is asserted, the direction of travel is the finding.
- **No halt verb exists in the document.** The strongest action language is preventing deployment following a failed acceptance determination. The RSP carries "we will delay"; OpenAI carries "halt further development" at Critical. The FSF carries neither: the only lab of the three with no written stop.
- **On-switch location: the deepest interior of the three.** The RSP suspends one real lever inside a semi-detached trust; OpenAI's strongest candidate is a committee of its own board; the FSF names no body at all. Descending exteriority across the cluster, ending here at an unnamed function. Nothing in the document routes any trigger call, threshold crossing, or acceptance decision to a body DeepMind does not appoint and cannot overrule; the sole external touchpoint (5.2 disclosure) is self-triggered voice.
- **The RAND security patch is the cluster's sharpest enforcement fragment,** and its scope is exactly one axis: weight theft. It makes the vault door externally benchmarked while the decision about what leaves the vault stays wholly internal. Sharp at enforce on security; nothing at decide.

*Cross-reference (separate source, per dive plan): DeepMind's later AI Control Roadmap (18 June 2026, not part of the FSF) contains in abundance the stop vocabulary this framework lacks -- shutdown, blocking, termination, incident-response playbooks -- aimed at DeepMind's deployed agents, not at DeepMind's decisions. The stop verbs exist at Google one level below where the on-switch question asks for them; see the reasoning note.*

## Who it binds

Google DeepMind only, voluntarily, by a document DeepMind amends through its own review cycle. No named accountable officer (the RSP has an RSO; OpenAI has a constituted SAG and a board committee; the FSF has the unnamed function). Binding force is reputational and political: the FSF is load-bearing in DeepMind's public safety positioning, which prices visible breach without providing any mechanism for one, and breach is harder to make visible here than at either comparator because the substantive obligations are self-referential (see the underdetermined bin) and the internal artefacts (safety cases, assessments, determinations) carry no publication commitment.

## Intersections with crisis / resilience

**Risk-assessment vocabulary without risk-assessment discipline.** The 1.3 sequence (identify, assess, mitigate, residual risk, accept) is standard doctrine and reads as such at the headings. Below the headings, measured against complex-environment practice (offshore safety cases, ICS, permit-to-work), every disciplining element is absent: no pre-existing acceptability criterion (the bar is written at the moment of determination), no independent acceptor (the author accepts), no duty holder, no compelled re-validation (change is self-detected: the process "detects substantial changes that invalidate our risk assessment", with no stated mechanism), and expert judgement invoked ("expert-driven estimates") without provenance or challenge. The safety case is the sharpest instance: the term is imported from regimes where an operator-authored case goes to an independent regulator with acceptance power, against criteria that pre-exist the assessment, with re-submission forced by material change. The FSF keeps the author and deletes the regulator, the criteria, the duty holder and the trigger. Post-Piper Alpha doctrine exists precisely because author-accepted safety cases failed.

**Self-clocked tempo, inherited, with the detector coupled to the detected.** The only calendar clock is the annual review; assessment cadence is self-set (alert thresholds placed "marginally" before CCLs, margin unquantified); and in the ML R&D domain the assessment may draw on DeepMind's own research progress, so the monitoring regime's response time is coupled to the thing it observes, the classic acceleration failure, here with the coupling written in.

**The escape valve opens onto the irreversible.** Running the reversibility instrument across the cluster: the RSP's hatch opens onto delay (fully reversible), OpenAI's onto loosened safeguards on a model it still operates (damaging, levers retained), the FSF's open-weights branch onto releasing the weights themselves (no levers afterwards: nothing recalled, patched, or un-released). The FSF sits at the bottom of the reversibility ranking by a distance, and the gate on its valve is one self-assessed benefit-risk judgement by the unnamed function.

**No incident commander, foundation edition.** The marginal framing makes every actor's duty relative to the others', the multi-agency accountability seam found at both comparators. The FSF's version is structurally worse: because the marginal judgement is woven into the standing acceptance criteria rather than confined to a hatch, the seam is not a failure mode the framework can fall into under pressure; it is the ground state.

**The 42001 contrast, operationally.** The inversion holds across a fourth framework: the FSF has thresholds, triggers and outcome substance where 42001 had none, and nothing third-party-attestable where 42001 was certifiable. The RAND reference is a benchmark, not an attestation; no third party verifies that SL2+ is actually met.

*Cross-reference (separate source, per dive plan): the AI Control Roadmap is the corpus's closest approach to genuine operational control doctrine -- insider-threat modelling on a MITRE-derived taxonomy, tiered detection and response, drills and playbooks -- and it concedes in terms that human-driven oversight may become too costly to maintain at AI speed and scale. It sits one level below governance, is not yet obligation of any kind, and is treated in the reasoning note, not merged here.*

## Open questions

- **Does the TCL tier catch accumulation, or just lower the single-event bar?** The two-tier structure is the cluster's first graded scope shape; whether it creates a genuine cell for compounding, sub-threshold harm or merely a smaller version of the same single-blow switch is untested in the text.
- **Who is the "appropriate governance function"?** The named-body question. Any future version that constitutes it (membership, appointment, tenure, escalation) materially changes the enforcement read; any version that names an external element in it would be the cluster's first on-switch outside a building.
- **Is "marginally before" enough?** Alert thresholds are placed at an unquantified margin before CCLs, in the one domain (ML R&D) where the tempo being detected is the lab's own acceleration. The adequacy of the margin is the whole question and the document does not state it.
- **The open-weights valve at future versions.** Watch whether any subsequent version adds criteria, a named decider, or an external check to the open-release branch at 1.3.5. Its current form is the cluster's least reversible act behind its least specified gate.
- **Does the Roadmap ever enter the FSF, and in what grammar?** DeepMind states the hope of incorporating the Control Roadmap once mature. If its measurable targets arrive as failable commitments, they would be the sharpest obligations in the cluster; if they arrive in the FSF's "we assess" grammar, the determination engine will have swallowed the one genuinely testable material DeepMind has written.
- **Does a state override reach DeepMind?** OpenAI met one (White House gating of GPT-5.6) and Anthropic met one (the Fable/Mythos export-control suspension). No DeepMind instance was found in this dive's checks; that is a not-yet-found, not a verified absence. A third instance would move the cluster-level pattern from developing to established.

## Cross-project hooks

- **The failability test resolved (synthesis T4; validation checkpoint 1 closes):** the FSF generates the underdetermined bin, confirming the third bin as a species feature and leaving the RSP the outlier as the only clean bisection. New structural content: two distinct roads into the bin (OpenAI, a missing standard; the FSF, a self-referential standard), and proof the bin can be a framework's centre of mass rather than its edge case.
- **On-switch location (synthesis T2):** descending exteriority across the cluster: semi-detached trust (RSP), own-board committee (OpenAI), unnamed internal function (FSF). Three labs, zero external on-switches; the FSF is the deepest interior. The Roadmap adds the twist: the stop verbs exist at Google, pointed downward at agents, not at the lab.
- **The pipeline (synthesis I1):** at coarse resolution a cluster (FSF joins OpenAI front-sharp and back-soft, against the RSP's soft front); at stage resolution a spread (three labs hollow at three different stages: define (RSP), decide (FSF), enforce (OpenAI)). The FSF's hollow is one stage earlier and deeper than OpenAI's, and decide is where the on-switch lives.
- **The reversibility asymmetry (I2, extended):** cluster ranking now three-deep: delay (reversible), safeguard-loosening on an operated model (levers retained), open-weight release (no levers). The direction the hatch opens matters more than how well it is guarded, and the FSF's opens furthest.
- **Woven versus discrete marginal logic (new for the synthesis file):** the comparators built marginal-risk hatches; the FSF poured marginal risk into the standing acceptance criteria. A hatch can be watched; a foundation cannot.
- **Honesty and discretion in the same clause (synthesis T3, third confirming instance):** "because AI security is an area of active research, we expect the concrete measures... to evolve substantially": the honest reading (defences must evolve as attacks do) and the self-serving reading (nothing is ever fixed) share the sentence, indistinguishable from outside. With the RSP and OpenAI instances, T3 is now a three-lab class behaviour.
- **The two-document split (new synthesis thread, from the reasoning note):** DeepMind alone divides its safety architecture into a model-level framework and a separate system-level control roadmap; both comparators keep everything in one document. The split concedes that model-level thresholds cannot carry deployment-time risk, and it sits directly on the seam gap (T8).
- **Template-setting loop (synthesis T6):** DeepMind raises the template at define (two tiers, the quantified ML R&D line) and at the security benchmark (external anchor), and lowers it at governance (the unnamed function, the backwards trajectory). The Frontier Model Forum terminology-harmonisation effort noted at the currency check is live evidence for the convergence half of the loop.

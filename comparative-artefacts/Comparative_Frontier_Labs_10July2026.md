## 1. Three documents, two shapes

The Anthropic Responsible Scaling Policy is the cluster's original and its fastest-moving document: four versions between February and May 2026, each self-amended. Its shape is a table of capability thresholds wired to safeguard levels, and its operative verb is delay: "we will delay AI development and deployment as needed" (Appendix A). That is the only stopping word the document contains, armed and disarmed by conditions the RSP itself defines. Around the table sits the cluster's most elaborate oversight apparatus: a Responsible Scaling Officer, Board approval gates, and the Long-Term Benefit Trust. Alone in the cluster, its obligations split cleanly into commitments an outsider could watch it fail and aspirations no one could.

The OpenAI Preparedness Framework v2 is the stable one, standing unrevised since April 2025 while the RSP iterated four times, and it is under live load: the GPT-5.6 family is designated High capability in both Biological/Chemical and Cybersecurity, so the v2 thresholds are being applied verbatim to deployed systems today. Its shape is the same species as the RSP's, Tracked Categories with High and Critical capability thresholds wired to safeguards, and it carries the cluster's only hard stop: at Critical, halt further development. Every determination, however, routes to a Safety Advisory Group that OpenAI's Leadership appoints, resources, and can overrule, and the one quasi-external body, the board's safety committee, has been tested against Leadership exactly once, in November 2023. Leadership prevailed.

The DeepMind Frontier Safety Framework v3.1 breaks the shape. It is not a threshold table with responses; it is a five-step risk-management sequence (Section 1.3) terminating in a Risk Acceptance Determination (1.3.5), with the threshold tables (Critical Capability Levels and, beneath them, the cluster's only second tier, the Tracked Capability Levels) reduced to inputs feeding that determination. At the front end it is the sharpest document of the three, carrying the most quantified single threshold any lab has written. At the back it contains no stop vocabulary of any kind: no halt, stop, pause, suspend, or delay appears anywhere in its text. That is a verified absence, not a characterisation. Its decider is an "appropriate governance function" that no section of the document constitutes.

So this is not a like-for-like comparison of three versions of one idea, and treating it as one would miss the finding. Two of these documents are trigger-centred: a line is crossed, a response follows. The third is process-centred: a determination engine into which the lines feed. Read the FSF as a threshold table and you understate it; read it as a determination engine and you locate the hollow. The comparative therefore runs on a structure none of the three documents owns, the four-stage pipeline every threshold-trigger framework instantiates: define the capability line, detect the crossing, decide the response, be made to act.

The corpus defines this species of framework by "capability lines, trigger machinery, decision logic". Set that definition against the pipeline and it maps onto define, detect, and decide, then stops. The fourth stage, enforce, is absent from the species' own anatomy. That absence, run down through three documents from three directions, is what this comparative keeps arriving at: genuine outcome substance, nowhere externally enforceable.

## 2. The spine: four stages, three labs

Every threshold-trigger framework, whatever its shape, is the same four-stage machine: define the capability line, detect the crossing, decide the response, be made to act. Running the three documents down those stages produces the cluster's central structural finding, and it reads differently at two resolutions. Coarse: two labs are sharp at the front and soft at the back, against one lab built the other way round. Stage by stage: the three labs are hollow at three different stages, so no two fail in the same place, and an overlay of all three covers the whole pipeline with hollows.

| Stage | Anthropic RSP v3.3 | OpenAI Preparedness v2 | DeepMind FSF v3.1 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Define** | **Hollow.** The cluster's softest lines | Sharp categories; the credited template | Sharpest: two tiers; the most quantified threshold written |
| **Detect** | Self-judged evaluation triggers; fuzzy off-cycle conditions | Tracked Categories evaluated; Research Categories named but untriggered | Middling: real machinery, self-set cadence, a skip valve, detector coupled to the detected |
| **Decide** | Board and LTBT gate: the most exterior lever in the cluster, still in-house | Routes to a Leadership-appointed SAG | **Hollow.** A self-certifying acceptance determination by an unnamed function |
| **Enforce** | Failable core: the strongest back end of the three | **Hollow.** Safeguards held to a standard that declares itself illustrative | Soft, bar one hard patch: the RAND-benchmarked security levels |

*Stage placements per the corpus four-stage pipeline instrument (I1), completed across the cluster 9 July 2026. Each lab's hollow marked bold; remaining cells are characterised, not ranked.*

### 2.1 Define: the cheap end, competed upward

The front of the pipeline is where the cluster is converging, and it is converging upward. OpenAI documents the inheritance in its own footnotes, crediting Anthropic's RSP for its report structure and Meta's framework for its Tracked-Category criteria. DeepMind then raises the template twice: the cluster's only two-tier threshold structure, with Tracked Capability Levels sitting below the Critical ones, and the most quantified single threshold any lab has written, the ML R&D automation line. Against that, the RSP's capability lines are the softest of the three, which is its hollow stage.

The convergence is real and it is also the least reassuring finding in the document, because definition is the cheap end of the pipeline. A precise line costs analysis; an enforceable consequence costs optionality. Precision without teeth is better-documented helplessness.

### 2.2 Detect: real machinery, self-set tempo

All three labs run genuine detection machinery, and in all three the machinery answers to a tempo the lab sets for itself. The RSP's evaluation triggers arm on determinations Anthropic makes about its own models, with the off-cycle disclosure conditions fuzzy enough that judgement has room to move. OpenAI evaluates its Tracked Categories on a defined footing but carries a second class, Research Categories, that are named and wired to nothing: sandbagging, a model deliberately underperforming under evaluation, sits in the framework as a recognised hazard with no trigger attached. The FSF's cadence is event-driven and self-set: alert thresholds placed "marginally" before the critical lines with the margin never quantified, assessments after significant post-training runs, and a footnote-4 valve permitting the assessment to be skipped.

The FSF adds the cluster's most structurally interesting detection problem: in the ML R&D domain, the thing being measured is partly DeepMind's own research trajectory, so the detector and the detected are the same organisation's progress. A detection system coupled to what it is detecting is not neutral instrumentation, and no compensating external check is wired to it.

### 2.3 Decide: the on-switch, and who holds it

Decide is where the operational question lives, because a framework's worth under pressure is decided by who can put it into effect against the operator's preference. The cluster answer is nobody. Every determination in all three documents routes through a judgement the lab makes about itself: each framework sits within the very mechanism it is trying to constrain.

The three on-switches sit at descending exteriority. The RSP's strongest lever runs through Board approval and the Long-Term Benefit Trust, a semi-detached body with real powers, still Anthropic architecture. OpenAI's determinations route to a Safety Advisory Group that Leadership appoints, resources, and can overrule; its one quasi-external body, the board's safety committee, has been tested against Leadership once, in November 2023, and Leadership prevailed. The FSF names no body at all: its decider is an "appropriate governance function" with no membership, appointment, tenure, or reporting lines anywhere in the document, and its sole external touchpoint, information-sharing with authorities at 5.2, is self-triggered voice.

The version history sharpens this from a snapshot into a direction of travel. Verified against all four published versions, the FSF's governance ran backwards: nothing in v1.0, three named councils in v2.0, the section and councils removed in v3.0, one unstructured paragraph in v3.1. It is the only framework in the corpus whose public governance became less specified as it matured. The corpus-wide grain, verified two-sided, is subtler and worse: across revisions the labs remove substance while accreting governance machinery. The RSP demoted its Automated R&D threshold to a checkpoint, made ASL-3 safeguards less prescriptive, and descoped sophisticated and state-compromised insiders, while expanding RSO duties and adding LTBT powers; OpenAI specified SAG composition and one-year terms. DeepMind alone removed the governance itself.

What the switch commands, when thrown, is the verb map that titles this document, established by word-boundary search of the three primary texts. The RSP's operative verb is delay: "we will delay AI development and deployment as needed", twice in Appendix A, with a third occurrence that disclaims it ("we will not necessarily delay") and a softer register ("we would strongly consider pausing") confined to preamble and changelog, outside the commitments. OpenAI carries the cluster's only hard stop, halt, three times, operative at Critical. The FSF contains no member of the family at all: no halt, stop, pause, suspend, or delay anywhere in its text, a verified absence. And the absence is a choice, not a house limitation: DeepMind's adjacent AI Control Roadmap is dense with stop vocabulary, shutdown, blocking, termination, drills, every verb pointed downward at the deployed agent and none at the lab's own decisions. DeepMind has designed a kill switch for its models and none for its decisions.

One development sits outside the documents and belongs in this stage at its held confidence, which is low: a developing situation, two instances. In June 2026 the state supplied the external switch no framework contains, twice inside a month: the GPT-5.6 flagship constrained to government-gated preview by the White House after crossing the High cyber threshold, and Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos 5 suspended by a Commerce export-control directive, lifted 30 June, both tracing to the 2 June executive order. No DeepMind instance has been found, which is a not-yet-found, not an absence. If the pattern holds, the on-switch the frameworks decline to build is being built anyway, from outside, and so far faster than any framework's internal machinery would have acted.

### 2.4 Enforce: substance without machinery

The last stage asks what makes the lab act when it would rather not, and the honest answer across the cluster is nothing external. The differences are in how close each document comes.

The RSP comes closest by construction: it carries a genuine failable core, obligations an outsider could watch it break, including ASL-3 safeguard maintenance, report cadence, the external-review conjunction, and the Board and LTBT approval gate. That makes it the strongest back end of the three, and still a self-enforced one. OpenAI's enforce stage is its hollow: Table 1 requires safeguards "meeting High standard", and the appendix that standard points to opens by declaring itself illustrative and not definitive, so the obligation dissolves in its own reference; its security appendix cites ISO 27001, SOC2, NIST SP 800-53 and FedRAMP, and attests to none of them. The FSF is soft at enforce with one hard patch, and the patch is instructive: its security levels are benchmarked to the external RAND weight-security framework, whose attacker-class definitions DeepMind does not own. That is the closest any frontier framework comes to external machinery, and it is a benchmark, not an attestation: no third party verifies the levels are met, the scope is one axis (weight theft), and it sits on internally adjustable footings, the 2.2 downgrade valve and the 1.3.5 branches. An externally defined bar, still internally judged.

Which lands the pipeline where the corpus keeps landing. The one framework in this project with real enforcement machinery, ISO/IEC 42001's certifiable third-party audit, wraps a management system with no outcome substance; the three frameworks with real outcome substance have no machinery. Substance without machinery is the frontier pattern, machinery without substance is the standards pattern, and nothing yet holds both. The field currently offers a choice between auditable emptiness and unauditable substance.

## 3. The obligations ledger

Strip any framework to its obligations and ask one question of each: could the lab demonstrably fail it? An obligation no outsider could watch being broken is an aspiration wearing commitment clothing. When the test was built against the RSP, two bins were enough: every obligation fell cleanly into failable commitment or unfailable aspiration. The second lab broke that. OpenAI's "safeguards meeting High standard" fits neither bin: real safeguards genuinely run, so it is no clean aspiration, but the standard they must meet declares itself illustrative, so nothing compels them and it is no commitment either. The test needed a third bin, and the corpus calls it underdetermined. The third lab then settled what the bin is: not an OpenAI quirk but a species feature, and one that can hold a framework's entire centre.

The RSP is the outlier, and creditably so: its obligations split cleanly into a real failable core (ASL-3 safeguard maintenance, report cadence, the external-review conjunction, the Board and LTBT approval gate) and an aspirational halo no one could fail. Its second-order pattern is less creditable: the control type degrades as the hazard gets harder to bound. Concrete hazards get real controls; emergent hazards get disclosure promises. That is the inverse of what a risk register wants, and it is the half-mitigation pattern from operational practice wearing frontier clothes: the half-mitigation recolours the cell from red to amber and stops the chasing without resolving the hazard.

The two roads into the underdetermined bin matter more than the bin itself, because they demand different detection. OpenAI's underdetermination is findable by following a reference: the bar points at an appendix, the appendix disclaims itself. The FSF's is findable only by grammatical analysis of the operative verbs, because there is no reference to follow and no standard to be missing; the obligations terminate in "we assess that" residual risk is acceptable, against no pre-existing criterion, decided by the unnamed function. An auditor checking only for missing standards passes the FSF, which has none to miss.

| Bin | RSP v3.3 | Preparedness v2 | FSF v3.1 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Failable commitments** | A real core | A real core | A thin, hard shell |
| **Underdetermined** | Empty | One occupant | The framework's substantive centre |
| **Unfailable aspirations** | The halo | The halo | The remainder |

*Three distributions of the same total softness. The underdetermined bin holds obligations that are neither failable nor clean aspiration: real machinery runs, nothing in the text compels it. OpenAI's route into the bin is a missing standard (a bar hollowed by the reference it points to); the FSF's is a self-referential standard (a bar written at the moment of determination, by the determiner).*

The FSF's column is the finding: population inversion, the middle bin holding not one stray obligation but the framework's substantive centre. The entire deployment-mitigation apparatus and the acceptance criteria it feeds (2.1.2, 3.1.2, resolving at 1.3.5) are real safeguards, genuinely operated, whose obligations end in self-assessment. And that resolves a question the whole cluster raises. A subjective, self-assessed obligation is not weakly failable. It is unfailable: any outcome is compliant. Subjectivity does not weaken an obligation, it dissolves failability altogether, and in the FSF the dissolved material is the centre. The failable residue is a thin, hard shell (the RAND-benchmarked security levels, procedural assessment duties, a safety case required at critical capability, the annual review) around a core that certifies itself.

## 4. The exits

Every framework in the cluster carries pre-authorised routes around its own obligations, exercisable under competitive or benefit pressure, all self-judged. The corpus calls these valves, and the discrete-clause kind, findable and watchable, hatches. The instrument that separates them is not how well-guarded each valve is but two harder questions: which direction does it open, and can what it releases be recalled?

The two hatches open in opposite directions. The RSP's Appendix A opens out of caution: its default state is delay, and the exit is the resumption of development, armed by evidence about competitors that Anthropic cannot obtain and disarmed by beliefs about itself. OpenAI's 4.3 opens out of the safeguards: its default is the safeguard level, and the exit lowers it. OpenAI's is the better-drafted hatch, carrying three guardrails where the RSP's carries fewer, and it is the better drafting on the worse move, because guardrails constrain the manner of a loosening, not whether it happens. Only reversibility constrains the consequence.

Reversibility is where the cluster separates by a distance. The RSP's default, delay, is fully reversible: nothing has left the building. OpenAI's loosened safeguards sit on a model it still operates: levers retained. The FSF's valve is the open-weights branch at 1.3.5, an alternative acceptance path under which a threshold-crossing model may be released as open weights on a self-assessed judgement that the benefits outweigh the risks. Open-weighting has no levers afterwards: nothing recalled, nothing patched, nothing un-released. The cluster ranking runs RSP, OpenAI, FSF, and the FSF sits at the bottom gated by one self-assessed call by the unnamed function.

The structural difference is worse than the ranking. The comparators' marginal-risk clauses are doors: discrete, findable, their invocation an event that can be seen. The FSF pours the marginal judgement into its standing acceptance criteria themselves: other developers' capabilities and mitigations are a live input to acceptability (1.3.5, and 2.1.2 factor iv), and the security levels are adjustable against the public floor (2.2). There is no clause whose invocation signals the marginal move, because the marginal move is the resting state. For anyone watching from outside, the consequence is exact: the least reversible act in the cluster is, in drafting terms, routine.

## 5. Under pressure: the crisis-resilience verdict

Everything above describes machinery at rest. The operational question is what happens when it is loaded: capability line crossed, competitor shipping, board divided, hours not weeks. Twenty years of emergency and crisis management reduce to one design principle for that moment: the decision must be cheaper than the deliberation. Incident command works because authority is pre-delegated to named roles, actions are pre-committed before anyone is frightened, and the whole arrangement is drilled until executing it is easier than arguing about it. Measured against that, the cluster's deciders are an unnamed function, an advisory group its overseer appoints and can overrule, and a trust one step removed from the operator. Not one framework names the person who throws the switch, times their decision, or drills the throw.

The clocks make the point without needing the doctrine. Across three frameworks there are exactly zero response clocks on any model: no deadline attaches to determining, deciding, or acting on a live system anywhere in the cluster. The FSF's only calendar clock is its annual review; its alert thresholds sit "marginally" before the critical lines with the margin never quantified. The RSP's 30-day disclosure clock is the only model-attached clock in the entire cluster, and it times a publication. The pattern generalises: these frameworks time the disclosure and leave the decision untimed, which is precisely backwards for crisis machinery, where the action is timed and the paperwork follows.

There is a citable counter-model, and it is not hypothetical. The UK offshore safety-case regime was written after Piper Alpha killed 167 people in 1988 and the Cullen Inquiry found that operator self-regulation, overseen by a conflicted regulator, had failed. Under SCR 2015, the successor to the regime Cullen created, the safety case is accepted by the competent authority before operation, not by its author; the acceptability criteria pre-exist the determination in the surrounding statutory regime; the duty holder is named; material change is barred until a revised case is accepted (reg 24); a thorough review is compelled five-yearly (reg 23); and the regulator can direct revisions (reg 25) and suspend operations (reg 24(7) to (9)). Set the FSF beside that and the comparison writes itself: the FSF is a safety-case regime in which the author accepts its own case, against criteria written at the moment of acceptance, by a function with no name. Offshore practice abandoned that architecture at the cost of a platform.

The deepest pressure problem is the one the labs can now see and have not built for. All three frameworks depend on the model's behaviour being characterisable: evidence in, judgement out. All three now acknowledge, in ascending strength, the hazard that kills that dependency: the RSP leaves it unnamed, OpenAI names it but wires it to nothing (sandbagging, a Research Category with no trigger), and the FSF promotes it to a tracked threshold (Stealth and Situational Awareness, a model that detects evaluation and behaves differently) and then wires the same self-assessed process to it. These are thresholds that mark the death of the machinery assessing them. The correct response to such a threshold is structurally different machinery: pre-committed responses, tripwires independent of the corrupted evidence, external evaluation. Ascending diagnosis, level treatment: three labs can see the failure mode, and none has built for it.

The verdicts, then, on the question the document asks. The RSP holds best under pressure and holds against no one: its default is the only reversible one in the cluster, its failable core is real, and every trigger that would engage them is a judgement Anthropic makes about itself, so what it holds is Anthropic to Anthropic's own reading of the moment. The Preparedness Framework holds if Leadership wants it to: the cluster's only hard halt sits behind a standard that declares itself illustrative and a body Leadership appoints and can overrule, and the one recorded test of that arrangement, November 2023, went to Leadership. The FSF does not hold under pressure because there is nothing in it to hold: pressure enters as one more input to an acceptance determination, and a process-centred framework under load does not break, it completes, with acceptance as the output.

As crisis machinery the cluster fails at the same point from three directions: when the moment comes, the decision is slower than the pressure, unowned by any named role, and reversible in only one document. These are serious documents built by people who expect the hazard to be real, and each one, examined at the point of maximum load, resolves into the same object: a well-instrumented description of a decision someone else will have to force.

## 6. Where each is sharpest

A comparative that only maps hollows flatters the analyst and misleads the reader, so the guardrail cuts the other way here, plainly stated, no hedging.

The RSP owns the back end. It is the only framework in the cluster whose default state under uncertainty is reversible, the only one whose obligations bisect cleanly into failable and not, and the only one carrying a genuine failable core an outsider could watch it break: safeguard maintenance, report cadence, the external-review conjunction, the approval gate. It also carries the most elaborate oversight apparatus of the three, and its trust, whatever its limits, is the most exterior lever any lab has built. Where the other two would need to construct enforceability, the RSP would only need to hand its existing machinery an external key.

The Preparedness Framework owns the hard stop and the honesty. It contains the cluster's only unambiguous halt verb, operative at Critical, where both comparators offer delay or nothing. It is the only document of the three that shows its working on inheritance, crediting Anthropic's structure and Meta's criteria in its own footnotes, which is template convergence documented from the source rather than inferred. And it is the stable one: unrevised since April 2025 while the RSP cut four versions in three months, a commitment that has not moved is worth more than one that might be amended before it binds, and OpenAI's is the only framework in the cluster currently proving itself under live load.

The FSF owns the front end. It carries the most quantified single threshold any lab has written, the ML R&D automation line; the cluster's only two-tier threshold structure, with tracked levels catching capability below the critical lines that OpenAI's single high floor cannot see; and the only commitment in the cluster benchmarked to an externally owned bar, the RAND weight-security levels, whose attacker-class definitions DeepMind does not control. One axis wide and internally judged, but it is the closest thing the cluster has to an obligation the lab does not get to define.

Assembled, the sharpest parts of the three would make one strong framework: DeepMind's thresholds, feeding OpenAI's halt, enforced through Anthropic's machinery with the key held outside. No lab has built it, but every component exists, published, in the cluster today.

## 7. What no lab has built

Four absences hold across all three frameworks, each verified in the corpus, stated here for the cluster only.

No external on-switch. Every determination in every framework routes through a judgement the lab makes about itself. Three labs, zero external triggers, and the one trajectory that moved, moved backwards.

No response clock. Not one deadline attaches to determining, deciding, or acting on a live model anywhere in the cluster. The only model-attached clock times a publication.

No structurally different machinery for legibility thresholds. All three now acknowledge, in ascending strength, the hazard that corrupts their own evidence base; all three wire it to the same self-assessed process that hazard would corrupt.

Nothing holds both substance and machinery. The corpus's one framework with certifiable external audit has no outcome substance; the three with outcome substance have no external audit. The field's current offer is auditable emptiness or unauditable substance.

These four absences are declared here and analysed no further: whether anything else in the corpus fills them is the gaps map's question, deliberately queued behind the NIST deep-dive. When that map is built, this section travels forward intact as its frontier-lab column.

## 8. What would change these verdicts

This document freezes; the frameworks will not. Five watch items, each attached to a specific verdict above.

Any lab externalising a single determination, an external body certifying a crossing, a constituted governance function with any external element, would be the first structural break in the on-switch pattern and would reopen section 2.3. The executive-order evaluation process has an August 2026 target: if it materialises and hardens into a standing regime, the external switch is being built from outside and T9 graduates from developing to structural; if it reaches DeepMind, the pattern completes. DeepMind states the hope of folding its Control Roadmap into the FSF: if the Roadmap's quantified targets arrive as failable commitments they would be the sharpest obligations in the cluster and the first structurally different answer to a legibility threshold; if they arrive in "we assess" grammar, the determination engine has swallowed the only genuinely testable material DeepMind has written. The Frontier Model Forum's terminology-harmonisation work will show its direction in its first published artefact: convergence toward the sharpest instance of each stage or the softest. And OpenAI's undefined Critical standard: the whole Critical tier, halt included, points at a standard that does not yet exist; the day it is written, the cluster's only hard stop either acquires content or is confirmed hollow.
